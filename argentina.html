Argentina

Background

Some communities in urban areas in Argentina are just starting to grapple with the humanitarian impacts of the climate crisis. As climate changes continue, weather related disasters and long-term hazard trends will continue, exacerbating existing vulnerabilities. An estimated 60% of disasters in Argentina are floods, which have resulted in 95% of economic losses for affected populations.

What communities say
People don't yet understand the long-term impacts of the climate crisis

People we spoke to perceived changes in the local climate, but they didn’t feel adequately informed nor knowledgeable about the long-term impacts in their community.

Awareness raising among communities and local authorities is necessary to strengthen local resilience.

Guilhermo
53 years-old, Neuquèn
We only become aware of climate change and so on when emergencies happen. When you see what can happen because of the rain ... and even then we don't get involved that much. Because you try to get out of it and that's it.
Communities and responders need to be more prepared, with stronger early warning systems

Despite increasing emergencies, such as floods, communities and responders are often unprepared. In Argentina, our work on climate action is just beginning—we need to support communities in setting up contextually relevant early warning systems, and ensure they have access to reliable information and data

Mauricio
Focus group discussion, Neuquèn
When the last floods happened, we started from scratch, without knowing. We were trying to organize in the best way, intuitively.
There is a lack of trust in emergency response services

A low level of community trust was perceived in some official emergency response services, a perception that was exacerbated by the ongoing election at the time of data collection. Communities criticized the performance of emergency services, particularly around the lack of involvement of the community and the alleged absence of emergency preparedness and response plans. The work and operational consistency of the Argentinian Red Cross was mentioned– this presents an opportunity to strengthen local capacities in emergency response, and to reestablish trust.

Maria
Key informant Interview, Corrientès
They [local authorities] didn't say anything, they didn't come or even assist us. They didn't even say what they were going to do or what they were going to do next.
Early warning information must be adapted to different contexts

Timely access to reliable and quality information is essential to help communities make decisions before an emergency occurs. In areas with low connectivity, digital channels are not effective to share information with local populations, and communities proposed to receive information via face-to-face, leaflets and other preferred channels.

Luìs
60 years old, Neuquèn
(…) the media tried to tell us that there was going to be a rise in the river's flow but we couldn’t understand what needed to be done(… There was a lack of information to be able to gauge what was going to happen. (…)

Context

In June 2023, heavy rains caused flash flooding in the Neuquèn province, affecting an estimated 4,000 families. The flooding caused power outages, road closures and contamination of water supplies—prompting the local authorities to request the support of the Argentine Red Cross.

In the Nother, heavy rain and flooding submerged the province of Corrientes on Nov. 6 2023. Many of the river islands in the region were completely swallowed, and thousands of people were forced to evacuate.
