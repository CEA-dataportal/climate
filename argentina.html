Background

Extreme weather events have turned southwest Bangladesh into one of the most climate-vulnerable regions of the Earth. The southwestern coastal Satkhira District of the country is alarmingly exposed to cyclones, and climate change-driven intrusion of sea water which damages land, crops and drinking water, and causes riverbank erosion. The Tangail District is situated in the influence zone of the Brahmaputra-Jamuna river system, which is one of the largest river systems in the world. Extreme heat and flooding are occurring more frequently than in previous years, causing great damage to agriculture and loss of property.

What communities say
Exacerbation economic crisis

Community insights tell us about how humanitarian impacts of the climate crisis are significantly exacerbated by an ensuing economic crisis. People flee a climate crisis, and are then faced with rising prices and lack of resources.

Recent changes in weather patterns affect farmers and force changes in how they make a living, showing that climate change is a complex problem that affects different people in different ways.

Guillermo
53 years-old, Corientes
(...) If you don't prepare, you don't prepare. But there are people who do (...) I thought it wasn't going to happen to me, until it did (...)
Precarious situation

To minimize the financial impact of the recurring loss of their homes, some people must make the hard choice of living in even more precarious situations.

Communities employ various strategies, relying on traditional coping measures, which are very limited. Coping strategies are consistent across age groups, underlining the crucial role of community support for collective efforts in facing disasters and access to resources for effective adaptation.

Community
Focus group discussion, Satkhira
I used to have four concrete houses, but due to riverbank erosion I have lost them all. Every few years I have to move my house to a new place. That's why now I only have a small shed made out of hay and straw, which is easier to move and much less expensive to build. Usually, here people keep dismantling their houses and shifting to a safe distance and rebuilding it, it’s the only way we deal with river erosion
Youth and Elderly People

The burden of the climate crisis largely relies on those middle-aged from the working class, who must provide for the young and the elderly.

Women group
Focus Group Discussion, Satkhira
The young and old cannot fully comprehend the effects of climate change, and so they are less able to adhere to community coping mechanisms. They are reliant on the middle-age working class for how to combat this which in turns elevates their already pressurised conditions.
More complex, More vulnerable

The climate crisis in coastal areas is driving migration to urban areas, where extreme heath and significant economic challenges create a parallel crisis, making many communities more vulnerable in the long-term. Factors like age, location, and income influence the level of concern, with lower-income individuals expressing more concern due to financial limitations.

Kareem
60 years old, Satkhira Sadar
Since cyclones are the single most destructive disaster for this area, the coastal and most vulnerable people often migrate to the city. But now heat has been rising significantly--for those who live in concrete buildings in the city it is already unbearable, I can’t imagine how hard it must be for low-income people who live in tin shed houses. The lack of resources in our city, which in turns causes the price of goods to rise continuously, is the biggest threat for Shatkhira to support the most affected communities.
Climate change information in the community

Community trust in climate information is diverse, ranging from reliance on expert knowledge and early warning systems to verification from peers within the community.

Although trust in local organizations addressing climate change is there, challenges persist about transparency in aid delivery from some NGOs. Internet access and information from community members contribute to overall trust in climate-related information.

Ashraf
40 years old, Restaurant manager, Tangail
Sometimes, we get the wrong information or the outcome of the predictions might not always be correct, but we still seek information the next time because we care about our lives and families.
